Present Simple :

The present simple tense is a tense that we can use for talking about daily routines , it's also used for talking about habits, general truths, or facts that occur regularly or are always true.

To conjugate a verb on present simple , you should use for :

  1. Positive Sentences : Subject + base verb (add -s/-es for third-person singular: he, she, it)
    • Examples :
      1. She loves writing
      2. I like staying at home
  2. Negative Sentences : Subject + do/ ( does for third-person singular: he, she, it ) + not + base verb
    • Examples :
      1. He doesn't like mathematics
      2. She doesn't like small talks
  3. Questions : Do/(Does for third-person singular: he, she, it) + subject + base verb.
    • Examples :
      1. Does she love painting ?
      2. Do you want to come with us ?

Present Continous :

The Present Continuous, also known as the Present Progressive, describes actions occurring over an extended period in the present or happening right now . it's often used to describe actions or events that are :

  1. happening right now or at the moment of speaking.
    • Example : She is reading a book.
  2. happening over a longer period in the present.
    • Example : They are working on a new project this month.
  3. Express future plans or arrangements.
    • Example : We are meeting them tomorrow.

To conjugate a verb on present continous , you should use for :

  1. Positive Sentence: Subject + am(first-person singular(I))/is(third-person singular(she,he,it))/are(second-person-singular(you), first-person-plural(we),second-person-plural(they)) + verb + ing
    • Example: I am studying English.
  2. Negative Sentence: Subject + am/is/are + not + verb + ing
    • Example: He is not watching TV.
  3. Question : Am/Is/Are + subject + verb + ing ? (Exception verb to be doesn't follow this rule)
    • Example: Are you coming to the party?

The Present Continuous tense is often used with certain time expressions that indicate actions happening right now, around the present time, or in the near future. Here are some commonly used expressions:

  1. For actions happening now or at the moment of speaking:
    • Right now
      • Example: She is studying right now.
    • At the moment
      • Example: They are discussing the project at the moment.
    • Currently
      • Example: He is currently working on a new design.
    • As we speak
      • Example: The chef is preparing the meal as we speak.
  2. For temporary actions or situations
    • These days
        • Example: I'm working from home these days.
    • At present
      • Example: At present, she is living with her parents.
  3. For planned future actions
    • Tommorow
      • Example: We are leaving for Paris tomorrow.
    • Next month/week
      • Example: They are moving to a new house next month.
    • Later
      • Example: He's calling you later.
    • This weekend
      • Example: I'm meeting my friends this weekend.

Present Perfect :

The Present Perfect tense is used to describe actions or events that have a connection to the present. It highlights the completion of an action at some point before now or its ongoing relevance to the present moment. We use it to describe :

  1. Actions completed at an unspecified time before now
    • The exact time of the action is not important or mentioned.
    • Example : She has traveled to Japan.
  2. Experiences
    • Refers to life experiences or achievements.
    • Example: I have never eaten sushi.
  3. Actions that started in the past and continue to the present
    • Often used with For and Since
    • Examples :
      1. We have lived in this city for ten years.
      2. He has been here since morning.
  4. Describe recent actions with present relevance
    • Often used with words like just, already, yet, and recently.
    • Examples :
      1. She has just finished her homework.
      2. I haven't seen him yet.

    To conjugate a verb in the present perfect tense, you should use:

    1. Positive Sentences: Subject + have/has + past participle
      • Example: I have completed the task.
    2. Negative Sentences: Subject + have/has not (haven't/hasn't) + past participle
      • Example: She hasn't seen the movie yet.
    3. Questions: Have/Has + subject + past participle?
      • Example: Have you visited that museum before?

Simple Past :

The simple past tense is used to describe actions or events that happened and were completed at a specific time in the past. we use it to describe :

  1. Completed actions in the past
    • I visited my grandma last week
    • Example : He studied all night
  2. Actions in Sequance
    • Example: She woke up, brushed her teeth, and went to work.
  3. Specific time references
    • we met at 8 p.m. yesterday.
    • The train left an hour ago.

To conjugate a verb on simple past , you should use for :

  1. Positive Sentences : verb +ed (for regular verbs) and for irregular verbs you should memorize them
    • Example : He went to work yesterday
  2. Negative Sentences : Subject + did not (didn't) + base verb
    • Examples
    1. I did not (didn't) go to the party.
    2. She didn't finish her homework.
  3. Questions : Did + subject + base verb ?(Exception verb to be doesn't follow this rule)
    • Examples
    1. Did you watch the movie?
    2. Did they arrive on time?

Past continous :

The past continuous tense is used to describe actions that were ongoing at a specific time in the past. It emphasizes the duration of the action or its interruption by another action. we use it to describe :

  1. Action in progress at a specific time
    • Examples :
    • At 7 p.m., I was cooking dinner.
    • They were watching TV all evening.
  2. Interrupted action (with simple past)
    • Examples :
    • I was walking to the park when it started raining.
    • She was talking on the phone when the doorbell rang.
  3. Background Description in Stories
    • Example : The sun was setting, and the birds were singing.

To conjugate a verb in past continous , you should use for :

  1. Positive Sentences : Subject + was/were + verb + ing
    • Example : I was working .
  2. Negative Sentences:Subject + was/were not (wasn't/weren't) + verb+ing
    • Example : They were not (weren't) talking.
  3. Questions: Was/Were + subject + verb + ing ?
      4. Example : Were you waiting for someone?

Past Perfect :

The past perfect tense is used to describe an action that was completed before another action or point in the past. It shows the "earlier" of two past events. we use it to describe

  1. Action completed before another action
    • Examples:
    • When I arrived at the station, the train had already left.
    • She had finished her homework before she went out.
  2. Action completed before a specific time
    • Examples:
    • By 10 p.m., they had eaten dinner.
    • He had saved enough money by the time he bought the car.
  3. Reported speech (past of the past)
    • Examples:
    • He said he had met her before.
    • They told us they had finished the project.

To conjugate a verb in the past perfect tense, you should use:

  1. Positive Sentences: Subject + had + past participle
    • Example: She had visited the museum before it closed.
  2. Negative Sentences: Subject + had not (hadn't) + past participle
    • Example: I had not (hadn't) seen the movie before.
  3. Questions: Had + subject + past participle?
    • Example: Had they completed the task before the deadline?

Forms

  1. Verbs
  2. Nouns :

    • Adds an apostrophe + s or just an apostrophe(for plural nouns).

Clauses

In English, clauses are groups of words containing a subject and a predicate. Clauses can be divided into two main types: independent clauses and dependent clauses.

  1. Independent Clauses:
    • Definition: Can stand alone as a complete sentence because they express a complete thought.
    • Example: She went to the market.
  2. Dependent Clauses:
    • Definition: Cannot stand alone as a sentence because they do not express a complete thought. They depend on an independent clause for meaning.
    • Examples: Because she was late..., When the rain stopped...
    • Types:
      • Relative Clauses: A type of dependent clause introduced by relative pronouns like who, whom, whose, which, that.
        • Example: The book that I borrowed is fascinating.
      • Conditional Clauses: Describe conditions and often use if or unless.
        • Example: If it rains, we will stay indoors.
      • Comparative Clauses: Express comparisons and are often introduced by than or as.
        • Example: She is taller than her brother.

Active and Passive Voice

In English grammar, voice refers to the form of a verb that indicates whether the subject performs the action (active voice) or receives the action (passive voice).

  1. Active Voice:
    • Definition: The subject performs the action of the verb.
    • Structure: Subject + Verb + Object.
    • Example: The cat chased the mouse.
  2. Passive Voice:
    • Definition: The subject receives the action of the verb.
    • Structure: Object + Form of "to be" + Past Participle + (by + Subject).
    • Example: The mouse was chased by the cat.
  3. Key Differences:
    • Focus:
      • Active Voice: Focuses on the doer of the action.
      • Passive Voice: Focuses on the recipient of the action.
    • Use Cases:
      • Active Voice: Preferred in most writing for clarity and directness.
      • Passive Voice: Used when the doer is unknown, unimportant, or needs to be de-emphasized. Example: The report was completed on time.

Direct and Indirect Speech

In English grammar, speech can be reported in two ways: direct speech and indirect (or reported) speech. They are used to convey what someone has said.

  1. Direct Speech:
    • Definition: The exact words spoken by someone are quoted, usually enclosed in quotation marks.
    • Structure: Subject + Reporting Verb + Quotation Marks (" ") + Exact Words.
    • Example: He said, "I am going to the market."
  2. Indirect Speech:
    • Definition: The words spoken by someone are reported without quoting their exact words, often involving a change in tense and pronouns.
    • Structure: Subject + Reporting Verb + That (optional) + Reported Clause.
    • Example: He said that he was going to the market.
  3. Key Differences:
    • Quotation:
      • Direct Speech: Uses quotation marks.
      • Indirect Speech: Does not use quotation marks.
    • Changes:
      • Pronouns: May change to suit the context. Example: He said, "I am tired." → He said that he was tired.
      • Tense: Usually shifts back. Example: Present → Past.
      • Time and Place: Words like *now* may change to *then*. Example: "I will go tomorrow." → He said he would go the next day.
    • Use Cases:
      • Direct Speech: Useful for exact quotes or dramatic effect.
      • Indirect Speech: Useful for summarizing or reporting what was said.
